## Quick Verdict

QuillBot is a solid paraphrasing and grammar tool that earns its place if your daily work involves rewriting, summarizing, or polishing English text — especially as a non-native speaker like me. It's not magic, and Claude can outperform it on nuanced rewrites, but the browser extension and one-click workflow save real time. For $9.95/month, it's a reasonable buy for writers, students, and ESL professionals.

## What is QuillBot?

QuillBot is an AI-powered writing assistant best known for its paraphrasing engine. It started in 2017 as a sentence rewriter built on early transformer models, and has since grown into a full suite that includes a grammar checker, summarizer, citation generator, plagiarism detector, AI detector, and a basic translator. Course Hero acquired it in 2021, and the user base has reportedly crossed 50 million.

In this QuillBot review, I'm focusing on how it actually performs in 2026 — not the marketing copy. I've been running it alongside Claude (my daily driver) and Grammarly for three months across blog posts, client emails, and a few academic-style pieces.

The core promise hasn't changed: paste text, pick a mode (Standard, Fluency, Formal, Academic, Simple, Creative, or Shorten/Expand), and get a rewritten version. What's different now is the depth of the surrounding ecosystem and how aggressively QuillBot positions itself against ChatGPT and Claude for writing-specific tasks.

## Hands-On Experience

I'll be straight: I bought Premium specifically to write this QuillBot review properly. The free version gave me enough to form opinions, but I needed to test the longer-form features and the AI detector.

**The paraphraser is the star, and it deserves the attention.** I fed it a 200-word paragraph from a client SaaS landing page that read too AI-generated (ironic, I know). In Fluency mode, QuillBot tightened the rhythm and replaced three robotic phrases with cleaner alternatives. I ran the same paragraph through Claude with a "rewrite for natural flow" prompt — Claude's output was better, but it took me three iterations to get there. QuillBot got close enough on the first try.

The synonym slider is genuinely useful. At minimum, you get light edits; at maximum, almost every other word changes. For my own writing, I keep it at about 50% — anything higher starts producing odd word choices ("commence" instead of "start," that kind of thing).

**Where QuillBot stumbles: technical content.** I tested it on a paragraph explaining JWT authentication. The Academic mode mangled "refresh token rotation" into "rotational refresh token systems." Not wrong, exactly, but worse. Anyone writing developer documentation should stick to Claude or just edit manually.

**The Grammar Checker surprised me.** As a Korean native speaker, my article usage is unreliable. I deliberately typed "I went to office to meet client" — QuillBot fixed both missing articles and flagged a clarity issue. Grammarly catches the same errors, but QuillBot's free tier doesn't nag you to upgrade every fifteen seconds.

**The Co-Writer workspace** is where Premium starts to feel worth the money. It combines paraphrasing, grammar, summarizer, and a basic AI writer in one panel. I used it for a 1,200-word newsletter draft. Workflow: write rough text → highlight clunky sentences → right-click paraphrase → accept or reject. It took maybe 30% less time than my usual edit pass.

**The AI Content Detector is the weakest feature.** I ran five samples through it: two written entirely by me, two written by Claude, one mixed. QuillBot correctly flagged one of the Claude pieces, partially flagged the mixed one, and gave a false positive on text I wrote myself at 2 AM. Originality.ai got 4 out of 5 on the same test. Don't trust this feature for anything serious.

**The browser extension** has been the unexpected hero of this QuillBot review. It works inside Gmail, Google Docs, LinkedIn, and most CMSes. Highlight text, click the QuillBot icon, get paraphrasing suggestions in a popup. I used it to fix awkward LinkedIn comments more times than I'd like to admit.

One annoyance: the extension occasionally double-loads on Google Docs and slows down typing. I had to disable and re-enable it twice during testing. Minor, but worth noting.

## Who Should Use QuillBot?

**Good fit for:**
- ESL professionals writing business English daily (this is me)
- Students rewriting research notes and managing citations
- Content marketers polishing first drafts at scale
- Bloggers who want a faster edit pass without firing up a full LLM
- Anyone who lives inside Gmail and Google Docs

**Skip it if:**
- You already pay for Claude Pro or ChatGPT Plus and can write good prompts — you'll get better results manually
- You write highly technical or developer-focused content
- You need a reliable AI detector (look at Originality.ai or GPTZero)
- You're a heavy Grammarly user — the overlap isn't worth two subscriptions

## Alternatives to Consider

- **Claude (Anthropic)** — My personal pick for nuanced rewriting. Better output, but requires prompting skill and a separate workflow. $20/mo for Pro.
- **Grammarly Premium** — Stronger grammar coverage and tone detection, weaker paraphrasing. $12/mo annual.
- **Wordtune** — Closer competitor to QuillBot's paraphraser. Cleaner UI, fewer extras. $9.99/mo.
- **ChatGPT Plus** — Same logic as Claude. More flexible, but you're paying for general intelligence, not a writing tool.
- **DeepL Write** — Excellent for ESL writers, especially European languages. Free tier is generous.

## Final Rating: 3.8 / 5

QuillBot does what it claims, charges a fair price, and stays out of your way. It loses points for the unreliable AI detector and weak performance on technical content. But for the target audience — writers, students, ESL professionals — this QuillBot review lands solidly on "yes, worth it." Just don't expect it to replace a proper LLM for serious creative work.

## FAQ

**Is QuillBot better than Grammarly?**
Different tools. QuillBot wins on paraphrasing and rewriting; Grammarly wins on grammar coverage, tone detection, and style consistency. If I had to pick one, I'd pick QuillBot for cost and the paraphraser. Many people use both.

**Can QuillBot bypass AI detectors?**
Not reliably. I tested Claude-generated text run through QuillBot's "Humanize" feature against GPTZero and Originality.ai — detection dropped, but didn't disappear. Don't use this tool to fake authorship; use it to genuinely improve your own writing.

**Is QuillBot free version enough?**
For occasional use, yes. The 125-word paraphraser cap is the main constraint — you'll hit it fast on real work. Grammar checker on free is solid. If you paraphrase more than 3-4 times a week, Premium pays for itself in saved time.

**Does QuillBot work for languages other than English?**
The paraphraser is English-only. The translator supports 30+ languages, and the grammar checker has limited support for French, German, and Spanish. For Korean (my use case), it's English-only — I write English drafts and use QuillBot to polish them.
