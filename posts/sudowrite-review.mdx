## What is Sudowrite?

Sudowrite is an AI writing assistant built specifically for fiction writers — novelists, short story writers, screenwriters, and serialized fiction authors. Unlike general-purpose LLMs like Claude or ChatGPT, it's been fine-tuned and wrapped in a UI that understands story craft: character arcs, scene structure, sensory description, foreshadowing, and pacing.

I started this Sudowrite review skeptical. I'm a daily Claude user, and my first instinct was: why pay for another AI when Claude writes beautifully with the right prompt? The short answer is workflow. Sudowrite isn't competing on raw model quality — it's competing on how fiction writers actually work. Outlines, character bibles, chapter drafts, revisions on specific paragraphs. That's where this tool earns its keep.

It's been around since 2020, originally built by Amit Gupta and James Yu, and has quietly become the de facto AI tool for indie novelists on Reddit's r/writing and various Discord communities.

## Key Features

- **Story Engine** — Multi-step outline-to-draft pipeline that maintains character and plot consistency across chapters
- **Write** — Predictive continuation that drafts the next 300 words in your style
- **Describe** — Generates sensory-rich descriptions (sight, sound, smell, metaphor) for any object, character, or scene
- **Brainstorm** — Lists ideas for character names, plot twists, dialogue lines, world-building details
- **Canvas** — Visual workspace combining notes, outlines, and draft text on one infinite board
- **Rewrite** — Rephrases selected text in different tones (more vivid, shorter, more intense)
- **Character Bible & Codex** — Stores characters, locations, and lore that the AI references automatically
- **Visualize** — Generates AI images of scenes and characters (Midjourney-style)

## Hands-On Experience

I tested Sudowrite by porting an existing 40,000-word draft of a sci-fi novella I've been working on between Claude sessions for the past year. This gave me a real baseline: I knew exactly how Claude handled these scenes, so I could compare.

**The Story Engine surprised me.** I uploaded my outline, character bios, and the first three chapters. Then I asked it to draft chapter four. The output wasn't perfect — the prose had that slightly over-adjectived "AI fiction" texture — but it remembered that my protagonist had a limp from chapter two, called back to a throwaway line about her sister, and matched the cold, clipped tone I'd established. Claude does this too, but only if I manually paste in context every session. Sudowrite just *does* it.

**Describe is the feature I use most.** I'll write something flat like "the market was crowded" and run Describe. It returns five options ranging from sensory ("the smell of fried garlic clung to her hair") to metaphorical ("the crowd moved like a single tired animal"). About 30% are unusable, 50% are decent, 20% are better than what I'd write on a tired Tuesday night. For a solo developer who writes fiction at midnight after debugging, that hit rate matters.

**Where it stumbled:** I tried writing a tense dialogue scene between two characters with conflicting motivations. Sudowrite's drafts were emotionally generic — characters explained their feelings instead of showing them through subtext. I ended up doing this scene in Claude with a long subtext-focused prompt, then pasting the result back into Sudowrite. So the workflow is hybrid for me.

**The credit system is the biggest annoyance.** I'm on the Professional plan ($29/mo, 90,000 credits). Generating one chapter with Story Engine costs around 8,000–15,000 credits depending on length. Add some Describe runs and Rewrites, and I burned through a month's credits in about 18 days. The Max plan at $59/mo gives you 300,000 credits, which is more realistic if you're actually drafting a novel.

**Canvas is underrated.** I dumped my entire chapter outline, character notes, and scene fragments into one canvas. Being able to see the whole novel at a glance — and drag scenes around — is something neither Claude nor Scrivener does well. I now plan all new chapters in Canvas before drafting anywhere else.

After six weeks, my honest take in this Sudowrite review: I haven't cancelled my Claude subscription, and I won't. But Sudowrite has earned a permanent spot in my fiction workflow. For non-fiction, blogging, or code-adjacent writing, Claude still wins easily.

## Who Should Use Sudowrite?

Sudowrite makes sense if you:

- Write novels, novellas, or serialized fiction (Royal Road, Kindle Vella, Wattpad authors love it)
- Already have an outline and want help drafting faster
- Struggle with description and sensory detail
- Work on long projects where context windows matter
- Don't mind editing AI-generated prose into your own voice

Skip it if you:

- Write literary fiction where every sentence matters (you'll spend more time fixing than writing)
- Write primarily in non-English languages (output quality drops noticeably)
- Already have a deep Claude or ChatGPT workflow you're happy with
- Are on a tight budget — credits add up fast

## Alternatives to Consider

- **Claude (Pro/Max)** — My daily driver. Better raw prose quality, but no fiction-specific UI. You build your own workflow.
- **NovelCrafter** — Closest direct competitor. More transparent about which AI model runs in the background (you bring your own API key). Cheaper long-term, steeper learning curve.
- **Novelai** — Better for niche genres and uncensored content. Worse for literary prose.
- **ChatGPT + Custom GPT** — If you build a good Custom GPT with your story bible, you can replicate 60% of Sudowrite for $20/mo.
- **Scrivener + Claude** — Old-school combo. No AI integration, but Scrivener's organization plus pasting into Claude works fine for disciplined writers.

## FAQ

**Is Sudowrite better than ChatGPT or Claude for fiction?**

For workflow and continuity on long projects, yes. For raw prose quality on individual passages, Claude (especially Opus) often writes better. I use both — Sudowrite for structure and drafting, Claude for polish.

**How many credits do I actually need to write a novel?**

Roughly: a 80,000-word novel with heavy AI assistance burns through 1.5–2 million credits, which means the Max plan ($59/mo) for 5–6 months. Light assistance (mostly Describe and Brainstorm) can get you through on Professional in 3–4 months.

**Does Sudowrite work for non-English fiction?**

It technically supports other languages, but quality drops sharply. I tested Korean output — it's grammatically okay but stylistically flat. For Korean or Japanese fiction, you're better off using Claude directly with strong style prompts.

**Will publishers reject my book if I use Sudowrite?**

This is changing fast. Most trad publishers now ask about AI use in submission forms. Self-publishers on Amazon must disclose AI-generated content but can still publish. Use Sudowrite as an assistant, not a ghostwriter — heavy human editing is both ethical and necessary for quality.
