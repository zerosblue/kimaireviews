## What is ProWritingAid?

ProWritingAid is a grammar checker, style editor, and writing coach rolled into one. It launched in 2012 and has slowly become the serious writer's alternative to Grammarly — less focused on flashing red underlines, more focused on showing you patterns in your writing you didn't know existed.

Unlike most AI writing assistants in 2026, ProWritingAid isn't trying to write *for* you. It's trying to make *you* a better writer. That distinction matters. I use Claude when I need to draft something fast. I use ProWritingAid when I want to ship something I'm proud of.

The platform works as a web app, desktop app (Mac/Windows), browser extension, and integrations for MS Word, Google Docs, Scrivener, and Obsidian. That last one is why I tested it — I write everything in Obsidian first.

## Hands-On Experience

I'll be honest: my first week with ProWritingAid was frustrating.

I pasted in a 1,800-word blog post I'd already published — a comparison piece on AI coding assistants — and the tool flagged 312 issues. Three hundred and twelve. Grammarly would have shown me maybe 40. My ego took a small hit.

But here's what changed my mind. I clicked into the **Sticky Sentences** report and saw a sentence I'd written: *"The thing about the way that most of these tools approach the problem of context is that they tend to make the mistake of..."* — yeah. Glue words everywhere. The report didn't just flag it; it showed me the glue word ratio (62%) and explained that anything above 45% reads as molasses. I rewrote it in 20 seconds and the new version was half the length and twice as clear.

That's the ProWritingAid review moment for me — the tool teaches you patterns. After a month, my first drafts started coming in cleaner because I'd internalized the rules.

**Where it shined in my workflow:**

I run a weekly newsletter for Korean developers learning English technical writing. I started running every piece through ProWritingAid's **Readability** and **Sentence Length** reports before sending. The visual histogram of sentence lengths is genuinely useful — I caught a section where I'd written eight consecutive 22-word sentences. No human reader would consciously notice, but they'd feel the drag. Fixed it. Open rate went up 4 points the following week. Correlation, not causation, but I'll take it.

The **Overused Words** report caught my personal tics. I say "actually" too much. I say "honestly" too much. I now do a 30-second pass with this report on every long article. The desktop app handled a 6,000-word document without breaking a sweat — though the browser extension stuttered on the same file in Google Docs.

**Where it disappointed:**

The interface looks like it was designed in 2019 and lightly refreshed. Coming from Claude's clean chat interface or Notion AI's inline suggestions, ProWritingAid feels cluttered. There are too many panels, too many tabs, and the color coding (blue for style, purple for grammar, yellow for...something?) takes a week to memorize.

The new **AI Sparks** rewriting feature is fine but underwhelming. I asked it to rewrite a paragraph in a "more conversational tone" and got a version that sounded like a stock LinkedIn post. I ran the same prompt through Claude and got something I'd actually publish. ProWritingAid is a better *editor* than it is a *rewriter* — know which job you're asking it to do.

Real-time checking on long docs (3,000+ words) gets sluggish in the web app. The desktop app handles this better, but I had to restart it twice during my testing period when it froze on a 10,000-word piece.

**The Obsidian integration is a hidden gem.** Most reviewers skip this, but if you write in Obsidian like I do, the community plugin works surprisingly well — you get the major reports without leaving your vault. It's not real-time, but on-demand checking is enough for my workflow.

## FAQ

**Is ProWritingAid better than Grammarly?**
For long-form writing, yes — the depth of reports is unmatched. For quick emails and short messages, Grammarly's interface is faster and more polished. I use both: Grammarly in my browser for daily writing, ProWritingAid for anything over 800 words.

**Does ProWritingAid work offline?**
Yes. The desktop app for Mac and Windows runs all major reports locally without uploading your document. This is one of the few writing tools in 2026 that genuinely respects offline workflows.

**Is the lifetime license worth it?**
If you write seriously for more than 3 years, yes. At $399, it pays for itself in roughly 3.3 years versus the yearly plan. I bought it after two months of testing and consider it one of my best software purchases of the year.

**Can ProWritingAid replace a human editor?**
No, and it doesn't try to. It catches mechanical issues, style patterns, and structural weaknesses — but it can't judge whether your argument is convincing or your story is moving. Think of it as a tireless first-pass editor that frees up a human editor to focus on what matters.
