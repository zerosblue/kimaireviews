## Hands-On: Sora

My experience with Sora is, admittedly, limited by its exclusivity. When I finally gained access to the sandbox, the first thing I noticed was the sheer physical intelligence of the model. I prompted it to simulate a glass of water being knocked over on a messy desk. The way the liquid interacted with the papers was staggering—Sora understands physics better than any other model I’ve tested.

However, the "Sora vs Kling AI" experience hits a wall when you try to iterate. If I want to adjust the camera angle by 15 degrees, I have to re-prompt and hope for the best. There is very little "steering" available. It is a generative powerhouse, but it treats the user like a spectator rather than a director. It’s perfect for artistic conceptualization, but for a developer looking to integrate video into a CRM-driven marketing automation, it’s currently a black box.

## Hands-On: Kling AI

Kling AI feels like a tool built for people who actually make things. I’ve been using it for a week to generate background assets for a SaaS landing page. The "Brush" tool allows me to mask specific areas of the frame—say, a hand holding a phone—and tell the AI exactly how that element should move. This level of granular control is missing from Sora.

When comparing **Sora vs Kling AI**, the standout for me is the stability of the output. I tested both with a prompt about a person walking through a bustling Seoul subway station. Sora gave me a more "cinematic" look, but Kling AI gave me a more *usable* one. The consistency of the character’s features from frame 1 to frame 60 in Kling AI meant I didn't have to deal with the dreaded "AI morphing" that often ruins deep-fake style video tasks. It’s this reliability that makes Kling AI my current primary choice.

### Does Kling AI have an API for my custom apps?

### Is the learning curve for Kling AI steep?
