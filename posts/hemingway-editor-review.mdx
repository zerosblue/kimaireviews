## Quick Verdict

Hemingway Editor remains one of the simplest readability tools you can use, and the free web version still earns its place in my workflow. But in 2026, with Claude and ChatGPT doing 90% of what the paid Plus plan offers, it's hard to justify the subscription. Use the free version, skip the AI upgrade.

## What is Hemingway Editor?

Hemingway Editor is a writing tool that grades your prose for readability. Paste in text, and it highlights long sentences in yellow, very hard sentences in red, adverbs in blue, passive voice in green, and complex phrases in purple. You get a grade level score (aim for Grade 9 or below for general readers) and a word count breakdown.

It launched back in 2013, named after Ernest Hemingway's famously terse style, and has stayed remarkably consistent since. The free web app at hemingwayapp.com requires no signup. There's also a $19.99 one-time desktop app and a newer "Hemingway Plus" subscription with AI editing features that started rolling out a few years ago.

This Hemingway Editor review is based on six months of using it alongside Claude for my own blog posts, client emails, and product copy here at kimaireviews.com.

## Key Features

- **Readability grading** — color-coded highlights for hard sentences, adverbs, passive voice, and complex phrases
- **Grade level score** — tells you what reading level your text targets
- **Word count and reading time** — basic stats most editors have
- **Format mode vs. Edit mode** — write distraction-free, then switch to editing
- **Markdown and HTML export** — handy for bloggers (desktop version)
- **Publish directly to WordPress and Medium** — desktop app only
- **AI Sentence Fixer** — Plus plan rewrites flagged sentences for you
- **Plagiarism checker** — included in Plus subscription
- **Offline desktop app** — works without internet, unlike the web version

## Hands-On Experience

I started using Hemingway Editor seriously about six months ago when I noticed my blog drafts were getting too verbose. As a non-native English speaker writing for an English-speaking developer audience, I default to long, clause-heavy sentences. Claude is great for drafting, but it doesn't naturally produce tight prose unless I prompt it carefully.

Here's how my workflow actually looks: I draft a post in Claude, paste the output into Hemingway Editor, and watch the screen light up like a Christmas tree. A recent 1,800-word review draft came back with 23 "hard to read" sentences and 14 "very hard" ones. Grade 12 reading level. Not great.

The visual feedback is the killer feature. I've tried Grammarly, ProWritingAid, and even Claude's own editing suggestions, but nothing makes structural problems as obvious as Hemingway's red and yellow highlights. You see the wall of color and immediately know which paragraphs need surgery.

That said, the algorithm is dumb in a frustrating way. It flags any sentence over roughly 14 words as "hard," regardless of whether the sentence actually flows well. Technical writing about CRM workflows or API integrations sometimes needs longer constructions, and Hemingway will scream at you regardless. I've learned to ignore maybe 30% of its warnings.

The adverb detection is useful but blunt. It flags "really," "very," and "actually" — fair enough, I overuse those. But it also flags "currently" in a sentence like "we currently support REST APIs," where the word does real work. You learn to use it as a checklist, not a rulebook.

I bought the $19.99 desktop app two months in, mostly because I wanted offline editing on flights. The desktop version adds direct WordPress publishing, which sounded great until I realized it hasn't been meaningfully updated in years. The interface looks like 2015. It works, but it feels frozen in time.

The big test was the Hemingway Plus AI features. I signed up for a month of the $10/mo plan to see if the AI Sentence Fixer beat what I get from Claude. It didn't. Not even close. The AI rewrites flagged sentences into shorter versions, but they often lose nuance or sound robotic. I'd paste a flagged sentence into Claude with "rewrite this for Grade 8 readability while keeping technical accuracy" and get noticeably better results every time.

The plagiarism checker in Plus is fine — comparable to what you'd get from a basic Copyscape check — but if you're writing original content, you don't need it. I cancelled Plus after one month.

Where Hemingway Editor genuinely shines in my workflow now: short-form content. Email replies, product descriptions, blog intros, LinkedIn posts. Anything where punchy matters more than nuanced. For a 200-word product blurb, Hemingway is faster and more focused than asking Claude to "make this concise."

Where it falls short: long technical articles, narrative writing, anything with intentional rhythm. The algorithm has no sense of voice. Hemingway himself would probably fail the Hemingway test on certain passages.

One small annoyance worth flagging in this Hemingway Editor review: the free web app doesn't save your work. Refresh the browser and your edits are gone. I lost a half-edited draft once because I closed the wrong tab. Now I always keep a backup in my note app.

## Who Should Use Hemingway Editor?

Hemingway Editor is worth using if you:

- Write blog posts, emails, or marketing copy for general audiences
- Struggle with run-on sentences or over-explaining (guilty)
- Want a quick second-pass tool that doesn't require subscription commitment
- Are a non-native English speaker who wants visual feedback on sentence complexity
- Need a distraction-free writing environment without paying for Ulysses or iA Writer

Skip it if you:

- Write fiction or narrative content where rhythm matters
- Need deep grammar checking (Grammarly is better)
- Already use Claude or ChatGPT for editing (you'll find Plus redundant)
- Write technical documentation that requires longer, precise sentences

## Alternatives to Consider

**Grammarly** — Better grammar engine, more thorough error catching, but the readability feedback is buried in premium tiers. $12-30/mo depending on plan.

**ProWritingAid** — Far more detailed reports than Hemingway, better for fiction writers, but the interface is overwhelming. $30/mo or $120/year.

**Claude (or ChatGPT)** — My honest recommendation for most editing tasks. Paste your draft, ask for specific edits (shorter sentences, clearer language, Grade 8 reading level), and you get contextual rewrites that respect your voice. $20/mo and replaces multiple tools.

**LanguageTool** — Free, open-source, decent grammar checker. Pairs well with the free Hemingway web app.

For my own work, I use the free Hemingway web app plus Claude. That combo costs $20/mo total and covers everything I need.

## Final Rating: 3.8 / 5

Hemingway Editor does one thing well: it makes readability problems impossible to ignore. The free web version is a permanent fixture in my workflow, and the $19.99 desktop app is a fair purchase for offline writers. But the Plus subscription is a hard sell in 2026 when Claude or ChatGPT do AI rewriting better as part of a broader toolkit. Use the free tier, skip the upgrade, and pair it with a real AI assistant.

## FAQ

**Is the free version of Hemingway Editor good enough?**

For most writers, yes. The free web app at hemingwayapp.com gives you the full readability highlighting, grade level scoring, and word stats with no signup required. You only lose offline access, direct publishing to WordPress/Medium, and the AI features — none of which are essential if you have another AI tool.

**How does Hemingway Editor compare to Grammarly?**

They solve different problems. Grammarly is a grammar and spelling checker that also flags some style issues. Hemingway Editor is a readability tool that ignores grammar almost entirely. Many writers use both: Grammarly for correctness, Hemingway for clarity. If I had to pick one, Grammarly catches more critical errors.

**Is Hemingway Plus worth the $10/month?**

In my testing, no. The AI Sentence Fixer produces shorter versions of flagged sentences, but the rewrites often feel robotic and lose nuance. If you already pay for Claude or ChatGPT, you're getting better AI editing as part of a broader subscription. I cancelled Plus after one month.

**Does Hemingway Editor work for non-English writing?**

Officially, no. The tool is built around English readability metrics, and the algorithm doesn't reliably analyze other languages. I tested some Korean text out of curiosity — it counts words but the grade level and highlighting are meaningless. Stick to English-language drafts.
