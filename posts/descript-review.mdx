Descript remains the most efficient way to edit video for people who think in terms of scripts rather than timelines. ## What is Descript?

If you are a developer, you’re used to manipulating text. You move lines of code, refactor functions, and use search-and-replace to fix bugs. Descript applies that same paradigm to video and audio. 

Instead of dragging a playhead across a complex visual timeline, you edit your video by editing the generated transcript. Delete a sentence in the text, and the corresponding video frames vanish. It effectively turns video production into a word processing task. As a solo developer in Seoul, I don't have the time to scrub through hours of footage. Descript has become my go-to for spinning up high-quality screencasts for my CRM projects without needing a background in professional film editing.

## Key Features

*   **Text-Based Editing:** The core engine that lets you edit video by deleting or rearranging words in the transcript.
*   **Studio Sound:** A sophisticated AI filter that cleans up sub-par audio recordings, making a bedroom setup sound like a pro studio.
*   **Filler Word Removal:** Automatically detects "ums," "uhs," and "likes" and allows you to delete them in bulk across the entire project.
*   **Overdub:** A voice-cloning feature that allows you to type new text and have it voiced in your own voice for corrections (useful when you forget to mention a technical detail).
*   **AI Eye Contact:** A clever tool that corrects your gaze in the video to make it look like you’re looking at the camera even when you’re reading a script.
*   **Screen Recording Integration:** Built-in capture tools that push directly into the editor for instant workflow.

## Hands-On Experience

I’ve been using [Descript review](https://descript.com) tools for years, but Descript feels different. As someone who spends his day in VS Code and Claude, I appreciate tools that respect my efficiency.

My primary use case is documenting CRM sales workflows. I’ll record a 20-minute screen share demo, often stuttering or repeating steps. In a traditional NLE, this would be a nightmare. In Descript, I open the transcript, search for the "um"s, and click "Remove all." Then, I look at the text and identify the sections where I drifted off-topic. I highlight those blocks of text and hit backspace. The video edits itself.

I often compare this experience to how I use Claude. When I’m stuck on a complex coding problem, I paste the snippet into Claude to get a clean, refactored solution. Descript does the "refactoring" for my video content. 

One minor gripe: I’ve noticed the app can get heavy on RAM. When I have multiple browser tabs open for my CRM testing and a large project loaded in Descript, my MacBook starts to heat up. It’s not a dealbreaker, but it’s something to keep in mind if you are running on an older machine. Also, while the "AI Eye Contact" feature is technically impressive, it can look slightly "uncanny" if you move your head too much. I tend to use it only for short clips where I really need to address the viewer directly.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I use Descript for offline editing?**
A: Descript is primarily cloud-based. You need an internet connection to sync your projects and utilize the AI features (transcription, Studio Sound, etc.). It’s not ideal for working on a plane without Wi-Fi.

**Q: Is the voice cloning (Overdub) feature ethical?**
A: Descript has strict safety guidelines regarding voice cloning. You have to verify your voice, and it’s meant for personal use. I’ve found it useful for fixing a mispronounced technical term, but it’s not for impersonating others.

**Q: Does Descript replace a professional NLE?**
A: For 90% of web content, yes. For cinematic projects or heavy VFX work, no. It’s a specialized tool for text-based narrative assembly, not complex visual compositing.

**Q: How does the transcription accuracy hold up with technical jargon?**
A: It’s surprisingly good. Because I work in CRM/Sales Ops, I use a lot of industry-specific terms. Descript catches these well, but you might need to occasionally correct a word in the transcript, which is as easy as editing a text document.
